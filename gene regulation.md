
## All of these listed below control gene expression within a cell. 
##### Transcription regulation: 
This is when RNA polymerase and other molecules must attach to a site upstream of the [[protein-coding gene]] region to start the transcription. 

#### Upstream 
-> [[gene control region]] : may also contain small segments of DNA where molecules can bind.


If a protein called [[repressor]] attaches to a site in that upstream region; for example, it will block the advance of the transcribing molecules, and the gene will not be expressed.


Other proteins, called [[transcription factors]], bind to sites called [[enhancers]] in the gene control region, where they activate gene expression. 

##### MicroRNA regulation: 
Silence genes by binding to mRNA molecules that would otherwise be translated into proteins. They prevent genes from being translated by binding to mRNA, leading to their degradation. [[MicroRNA]] molecules can also leave their cell of origin and make their way to distant cells.

They act like switches, turning genes on and off in response to changes in the environment. Some RNA molecules help to coordinate the development of embryos. For a human embryo to develop different tissues and organs, for example, certain genes must make proteins in certain cells while other genes must be blocked.

## However, there is also the case where a molecule made in one cell can travel to a different cell and still control its gene expression.

Eukaryotic cells can not only regulate the expression of a gene but also the kind of protein that is translated from a particular gene.

##### RNA Splicing 

After a cell transcribes a gene, it cuts out the introns from the RNA in a process known as [[RNA splicing]]. The resulting strand of mRNA contains only information from the gene’s exons.

The coding regions are broken up into segments called [[exons]]. 
Eukaryotic genes contain introns and exons. A group of proteins removes introns before translation, and it can remove some exons as well. By regulating which exons are included in the final mRNA transcript, cells can generate many proteins from a single gene. This process is called [[alternative splicing]].

The modular structure of exons and introns gives eukaryotic genes powerful flexibility.
