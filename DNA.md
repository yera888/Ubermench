#### First step, Replication 

When a cell divides -> it makes a new copy of its DNA -> **DNA-copying step that happens before a cell divides**.

- **S phase (S = synthesis):** [[DNA replication]] happens here. The cell copies its DNA so there will be two full copies.
    
- **M phase (mitosis + cytokinesis):** **cell division** happens here. The cell splits and each daughter cell gets one DNA copy.
    
- **You start with 1 double-stranded DNA molecule**  
    That’s **2 strands**: one “top” and one “bottom,” already complementary.
    
- **They get pulled apart** (unzipped)  
    Now you temporarily have **2 single strands** (each is an “original” strand).
    
- **Each original strand gets a new partner built onto it**  
    Enzymes add nucleotides to make the complementary strand for each template.
- You begin with **2 strands total** (1 double helix).
    
- After replication, you have **4 strands total**, arranged as **2 double helices**:
    
    - DNA molecule #1 = **1 old strand + 1 new strand**
        
    - DNA molecule #2 = **1 old strand + 1 new strand**
        

This is called **semi-conservative replication**: each “daughter” DNA keeps **one original** strand.

### Are the two new DNA molecules “the same”?

They are **(usually) identical in sequence** to the original DNA molecule (same genetic information), except for rare copying errors (mutations).

### Your “2 → 4” intuition, cleaned up

- Not: “the complementary strand formation makes 2 identical strands and then another 2…”
    
- Instead: “each of the 2 original strands gets a new complementary strand, producing **two complete double-stranded DNA molecules**.”
### Quick summary

- **Replication = copying DNA**
    
- **Division = splitting the cell**
    
- Replication usually happens **once per cell cycle, before division**, so the cell has two complete DNA sets ready to distribute.

DNA is kind of like the digital storage medium in a computer or a smartphone. 

In a computer hard drive, information is stored in a string of bits, which can be either one or zero. In DNA, there are four states for each position. As long as an organism can ensure that it copies each position faithfully, it can reproduce the entire genetic cookbook for a new organism.

On rare occasions, however, errors are introduced during copying. These mistakes, known as [[mutations]], are then transmitted each time the cell’s descendants make new copies of their DNA.
