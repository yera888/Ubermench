Chromosomes can occur alone, in duplicate, triplicate, or even higher combinations of homologous sets [[ploidy]].

[[autosomes]]

Because information in chromosomes is present in duplicate, a deleterious mutation affecting gene expression or activity in one chromosome may be masked by the presence of a functional copy of the gene in the other.
->
You usually have **two copies of each gene** (one on each of a pair of chromosomes—one from mom, one from dad).

- You have **two sets of chromosomes** in almost every cell.
    
- One set came from your **mom**, one set came from your **dad**.
    

### “Two copies of each gene” means this

Your chromosomes come in **pairs** (called _homologous chromosome pairs_).

- In humans: **46 chromosomes total = 23 pairs**
    
- For each pair:
    
    - one chromosome is the “mom version”
        
    - the other is the “dad version”
        

Those two chromosomes carry **the same list of genes in the same locations**, like two copies of the same book.

So for a given gene (say, a gene involved in eye pigment):

- you have **one copy (allele)** of that gene on mom’s chromosome
    
- and **one copy (allele)** on dad’s chromosome
    

They’re the “same gene,” but they can be **slightly different versions** (different alleles).

### Important exceptions

- **Sex chromosomes:** if you’re XY (typical male), many genes on the X chromosome don’t have a matching copy on the Y → you effectively have **one copy** for those.
    
- **Mitochondrial DNA:** comes mostly from mom, and isn’t paired like the nuclear chromosomes.
    


If **one copy gets a bad mutation** and doesn’t work right, the **other copy might still work**.

So the working copy can **“cover up”** the broken one, and you might **not see any problem**.

**Analogy:** You have two headlights. If one burns out, you can still see because the other one is still on.

###### Women

all 23 chromosomes form matching pairs, which include two X chromosomes

###### Men 

however, 22 pairs match, but the remaining two chromosomes are a single X chromosome and another one called the Y chromosome.
